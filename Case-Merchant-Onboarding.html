Act II · The Realization

Users were ready.
The product wasn’t.

Users weren’t confused. They weren’t hesitant. They weren’t price-sensitive.

They were already behaving like customers.

They compared vendors. They explored integrations. They evaluated outcomes. The signals of intent were everywhere — across nine different surfaces.

But the product treated those behaviors as passive activity, instead of what they actually were: buying signals.

Fig 02 Intent existed. The system ignored it.
High-intent behavior was distributed across the product. The upgrade path was centralized in a place users didn’t need to go.

We didn’t have a traffic problem. We didn’t have a pricing problem.

We had a translation failure.

The product could observe intent.
It just couldn’t convert it.

The assumption was simple: users would navigate to a dedicated pricing surface when they were ready to upgrade.

Reality was simpler: they never needed to.

The moment of intent was already happening — inside comparison, inside setup, inside usage.

The system wasn’t missing users.

It was missing the moment.

Act III · The System

Each behavior advances the system forward.

Funnels are not flows. They are state systems.

Every action moves the product into a new state. Every state must move revenue forward.

If it doesn’t, the state shouldn’t exist.

Act IV · System Rewrites

The system wasn’t improved.
It was rewritten.

Rewrite 01

Entry became a state, not a step.

The system stopped asking for commitment upfront. Instead of “start a plan,” users could experience value immediately.

Context — company size, usage, configuration — was captured progressively, only after the product had proven itself.

Entry was no longer a conversion event. It became the first state in the system.

Shift · signup → activation without commitment
Rewrite 02

Pricing aligned to behavior, not plans.

Static tiers were replaced with a usage-driven model. The more the product was used, the more it revealed.

Services became additive. Upgrades became automatic. There was no moment of renegotiation — only progression.

Pricing stopped being a decision users had to make. It became a consequence of how they used the product.

Shift · plan selection → usage-based progression
Rewrite 03

Monetization moved to where intent lives.

Upgrade opportunities were relocated from a single pricing page to the surfaces where users were already making decisions.

Vendor Comparison. Integrations. Analytics.

Locked features became live previews. Every high-intent interaction exposed the next level of capability.

The system no longer asked users to go upgrade. It upgraded them in context.

Shift · centralized pricing → contextual progression
Rewrite 04

The lifecycle became state-driven.

Every user action updated a system state: new, activated, engaged, expanding, dormant.

Each state triggered the next best action — not through campaigns, but through the product itself.

Re-engagement happened where the user left. Expansion happened where usage peaked.

The system became self-aware.

Shift · manual lifecycle → behavior-driven system
